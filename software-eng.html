Chapter 7: Software Engineering

Term: Definition:
Software Engineering: Definition: The branch of computer science that seeks principles to guide the development of large, complex software systems.
Integrated Development Environment (IDE): Definition: Combines tools for developing software (editors, compilers, debugging tools) into a single, integrated package.
Association for Computing Machinery (ACM): Definition: Founded as an international scientific and educational organization dedicated to advancing the arts, sciences, and applications of information technology.
Purpose of ACM: Definition: Promotes computer research and innovation through its journals, magazines, and the proceddings of more than 170 annual conferences and symposia.
Benefits of ACM Membership: Definition: Access to critical research, opportunity to establish a personal networking system, advancing your career and enriching your knowledge, reduced collective cost for faculty members of academic dapartments.
Student ACM Membership: Definition: Includes all of the benefits of student membership plus electronic subscriptions to CACM and XRDS, Yearly Rate: $19.00 (USD).
Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE): Definition: Organization of electrical, elctronics, and manufacturing engineers that was formed as the result of merging the AIEE and the IRE.
Purpose of IEEE: Definition: Purpose is to foster technological innovation and excellence for the benefit of humanity.
Benefits of IEEE Membership: Definition: Network with other technology professionals, establish a professional profile, join and participate in discussions on various technical interests, and create a group to share and collaborate on projects.
Student IEEE Membership: Definition: join a community of technology and engineering professionals united by a common desire to learn, interact, collaborate, and innovate, get the resources and opportunities you need to keep on top of changes in technology.
Software Life Cycle: Development Phase: Definition: The major steps in the traditional software development life cycle are requirements analysis, designs, implemtation, and testing.
Requirements Analysis: Definition: The goal is to specify what services the proposed system will provide, to identify any conditions on those services, and to define how the outside world will interact with the system.
Design: Definition: Whereas requirements analysis provides a description of the proposed software product, design involves creating a plan for the construction of the proposed system.
Implementation: Definition: Implementation involves the actual writing of programs, creation of data files, and development of databases, can see the distinction between the tasks of a software analyst and a programmer.
Testing: Definition: Testing is essentilly equated with the process of debugging programs and confirming that the final software product was compatible with the software requirements specification.
Modular Implementation: Definition: A logical partitioning of software design that allows complex software to be manageable for the purpose of implementation and maintenance.
Unified Modeling Language (UML): Definition: A standardized visual language that enables software developers to specify, visualize, construct and document artifacts of a software system.
Use Case: Definition: A methodology used in system analysis to identify, clarify, and organize system requirements.
Use Case Diagram: Definition: Depicts the proposed system as a large rectangle in which interactions (called use cases) between the system and its users are represented as ovals and users of the system (called actors) are represented as stick figures.
Data Relationships: Definition: Where two or more tables contain related data, and you've configured your database to recognize (and enforce) that fact.
One-to-One: Definition: Exemplified by the association between patients and occupied private rooms in that each patient is associated with only one room and each room is associated with only one patient.
One-to-Many: Definition: Exemplified by the association between physicians and patients in that one physician is associated with many patients and each patient is associated with one (primary) physician.
Many-to-Many: Definition: Would occur if we included consulting physicians in the physician-patient relationship, then each physician could be associated with many patients and each patient could be associated with many physicians.
Design Pattern: Definition: A predeveloped model for solving a recurring problem in software design.
Software Quality Assurance (SQA): Definition: Charged with overseeing and enforcing the quality control systems adopted by the organization.
Software Testing: Definition: The process of verifying and validating whether a software or application is bug-free and meets the technical requirements as guided by its design and development.
Glass Box Testing: Definition: The software tester is aware of the interior structure of the software and used this knowledge when designing the test.
Black Box Testing: Definition: Tests that do not rely on knowledge of the software's interior composition, performed from the user's point of view.
Beta Testing: Definition: A preliminary version of the software is given to a segment of the intended audience with the goal of learning how the software performs in real-life situations before the final version of the product is complete.
Alpha Testing: Definition: Similar to Beta Testing that is performed at the developer's site.
Documentation: Definition: Serves three purposes, leading to three categories of documentation: user documentation, system documentation, and technical documentation.
User Documentation: Definition: Purpose is to explain the features of the software and describe how to use them, intended to be read by the user of the software and is expressed in the terminology of the application.
System Documentation: Definition: Purpose is to describe the software's internal composition so that the software can be maintained later in its life cycle.
Technical Documentation: Definition: Purpose is to describe how a software system should be installed and serviced (such as adjusting operating parameters, installing updates, and reporting problems back to the software's developer).
Intellectual Property Law: Definition: Legal regulation of the right to control the use or dissemination of ideas or information that are the products of the imagination and intellect.
Software License: Definition: A legal agreement between the owner and user of a software product that grants the user certain permissions to use the product without transferring ownership rights to the intellectual property.
Software Disclaimer: Definition: Used in the software license that state the limitations of their liability.
Best Defense Against Software Liability Claims: Definition: Apply sound software engineering principles during the software's development, to use a level of care compatible with the software's application, and to produce and maintain records that validate these endeavors.
